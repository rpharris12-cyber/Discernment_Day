General Interview Tips

Use church examples, not just work onesAdvisers will probe this — they've seen executive candidates who know how to present competence. They want to know you in a church context.
Be concrete, not theoreticalEvery abstract statement needs a story behind it. "I try to create space" → "At the men's breakfast, a man said to me..."
Pause before answeringYou think quickly. A pause shows you're genuinely considering the question, not performing a prepared answer.
It's fine to say "I don't know"Especially on theological questions. Intellectual honesty is a quality they're testing for.
Don't let them make you crossThey will push hard on some questions. Steadiness under pressure is what they're testing, not your position.

Section by Section

45 sec — Intro

Some of you may have seen Clarkson's Farm. Jeremy Clarkson — best known for fast cars and loud opinions on Top Gear — bought a farm in the Cotswolds. When his farm manager retired, he decided to run it himself. He buys a huge tractor, hires a young local farmer called Kaleb, and confidently sets about becoming a farmer. The programme is very funny. But what makes it interesting isn't just the farming. It's that Clarkson slowly begins to change.

1 min — Farm changes him

Because out there, the land doesn't care who you are. The weather doesn't negotiate. The crops don't grow faster because you're impatient. Standing beside him is Kaleb — a young farmer who has grown up working the land — quietly explaining what Jeremy needs to do. And Clarkson has to listen. Slowly he begins to realise how hard farmers work, how fragile food production is, how dependent farming is on the land, the seasons, and other people. The farm begins to change him. Perhaps that's why Jesus so often spoke about fields, seeds and harvest. The land has a way of teaching things that other places can't.

1 min — Paul on Damascus

The Bible is full of stories of people whose lives were changed when they encountered something bigger than themselves. One of the most famous is Saul of Tarsus — and his story feels surprisingly familiar once you know Clarkson's. Saul was confident, driven, absolutely certain he was doing the right thing. He wasn't farming — he was persecuting Christians. He thought he had God on his side. And then, on the road to Damascus, he encountered the actual Jesus. Not a teaching about Jesus. Not a theological argument. An encounter. Something he didn't expect, in a place he didn't expect it. And it stopped him completely. His whole framework for understanding the world — everything he'd built his identity around — was challenged in a moment. He had to listen, just as Clarkson had to listen to Kaleb. The persecutor became the preacher. Saul became Paul.

40 sec — The thread

Both stories point to something simple. Transformation often begins when we encounter something — or someone — that challenges the way we see the world. For Clarkson it was the land and a young farmer who understood something he didn't. For Saul it was an encounter with the living Christ. In both cases their perspective shifted first. Then their lives followed.

20 sec — Scripture

Paul himself writes in 2 Corinthians 5:17: "If anyone is in Christ, the new creation has come: the old has gone, the new is here." Transformation isn't something we manufacture. It's something we receive.

30 sec — Reflective close

To be honest, my opinion of Jeremy Clarkson has changed watching this programme. Someone I'd completely written off — a loud, self-assured petrol head — turned into an unlikely farming ambassador. And that in itself is part of the message. Clarkson thought the farm was a project. It turned out the farm was the teacher. I wonder sometimes whether God works like that in our lives — through places, people or experiences we never expected to learn from. We don't transform ourselves. We get transformed.

Significant Life Events

Growing up in a home where faith was present but sometimes carried tension gave me an early and honest awareness of the complexity of belief. My mother and sister attended church regularly; my father had fallen out with the church following a disagreement connected with his wedding, and so faith in our household was real but not straightforward. Looking back, this shaped my understanding that people’s experiences of church are rarely simple, and that relationships with faith are often more complicated than they appear from the outside.
As a teenager I was part of a vibrant, Spirit-filled church in Wolston near Rugby. Those years gave me a real grounding in faith, and planted seeds that remained even through later seasons of questioning.
During my time at university in Hull, I stepped back from regular church attendance. The Church of England was in the midst of the debate about the ordination of women as priest, and a sermon I heard on the subject did not sit comfortably with me. I couldn’t find what I had at Wolston so my faith became quieter and more questioning during this period, though I remained loosely connected through bell ringing. Looking back, this was a formative time that helped develop a more reflective and honest faith rather than an inherited one.
In September 1997 I married my wife, Rosie. We had met some years earlier at university and she has been the most consistent influence on my faith journey. Our family grew quickly, with our sons arriving in December 1997 and September 1999 respectively. Those early years of family life brought real responsibility and grounding. Rosie’s steady and faithful commitment to church life was instrumental in drawing me back into regular worship and community after my period of distance, and her influence on my faith has been quiet, consistent and profound.
A close friendship that has shaped me significantly is with a friend met through mountaineering. In 2016 we completed the Cuillin Ridge on the Isle of Skye together, raising money for Christian Aid. During the climb a rock gave way beneath my foot and tumbled hundreds of metres down the mountain — and instead of panic I felt a profound sense of peace. At the end of the climb a plaque bore the words of Psalm 121: ‘He will not let your foot slip.’ When we returned five years later we began differently — with prayer. That shift felt significant. We have continued to study the Bible and encourage one another in faith ever since.
The experience of raising our sons deepened my engagement with church life. A significant turning point came when they attended Spring Harvest as teenagers and returned visibly changed — bringing the work of the Holy Spirit back into our home in a new and tangible way. In many ways it was the younger generation leading the older, and it prompted a deeper re-engagement with my own faith.
During a visit to our son Peter’s church in Plymouth, I experienced an unexpected moment of peace and encounter with God during prayer that I have not forgotten. It left me with a deeper sense that faith is not primarily something to understand, but something to receive — and then to give away.
Since early 2021 my wife Rosie has lived with a chronic pain condition that has significantly affected her life and, at times, our family. There have been periods when I have been her primary carer, and her condition continues to fluctuate. This has been one of the more demanding and humbling experiences of my adult life, requiring patience, adaptability and a deeper reliance on God than I might otherwise have found. It has also deepened my empathy for those living with ongoing difficulty and for those who care for them — in ways that I believe will shape how I approach pastoral situations.
In February 2025, due to health and mobility issues, my parents moved into assisted living near my sister. Since then both my mother and father have begun attending the local church — one they can walk to, where they are warmly welcomed. My father, after more than fifty years of distance from the Church, has found his way back to faith. Watching that happen has been one of the most moving experiences of my life. It is a reminder that God’s patience is longer than ours, and that no door stays permanently closed.
In July 2024 I became a grandfather for the first time. Our second grandson arrived ten weeks early in an emergency birth in 2025 — our first response was to get on our knees and pray. He is doing well, and a third grandchild is due in May 2026. These experiences have deepened my awareness of faith passed across generations and of trusting God in moments of real fear. I am deeply grateful to have a faith-filled family, and more aware than ever of how faithfully God works in ways and timescales we cannot predict.

Call to Ministry

Responds to Christ's call · Understands LLM ministry · Committed to public ministry in God's world · Has a sense of lay call

My sense of call has developed gradually but persistently — not through a single moment but through a growing recognition that God has been drawing together my faith, experience, gifts and opportunities in a particular direction. I understand Reader / LLM ministry as lay, public and collaborative, connecting church life and everyday life in ways that resonate deeply with how God has already been shaping me.
A key part of that calling is helping people become confident in their faith in the real world. During Covid I became aware that many Christians carry genuine faith but lack confidence in speaking about it — and that if a congregation can’t talk to each other about faith, how can they engage confidently in the world? I have felt called to help people become rooted enough in Christ to speak naturally and live faithfully in their everyday settings — what I describe as becoming radiators of Christ, carrying warmth and light into the places they already inhabit.
I feel called to remain in the real world and bring it before God, not withdraw from it. My workplace is central to this. In a senior technology role at Nationwide I lead complex programmes at the frontier of technology, including artificial intelligence — technology that is reshaping how people work, think and relate to one another at a pace the world — and the Church — have rarely had to face before. That experience has given me both a deep appreciation of how disorienting rapid change can be, and a conviction that Christians have not just a role but a responsibility in these spaces. AI is, in many ways, a sophisticated simulation of the past — it learns from what has already been said, written and done, and reflects it back. It has no moral agency, no capacity for love or hope or genuine relationship. Christians, formed by a living Christ, bring what AI cannot: conscience, presence, and a vision of the human person rooted in something far greater than data. That is why Christian faith needs to be present in these conversations, confident and clear.
Teaching is also central to my call. I feel drawn not simply to pass on information but to help people engage scripture, ask honest questions and grow into a faith that is truly their own — especially the next generation, navigating a world of competing voices.
I believe this call is to lay ministry specifically. Reader ministry reflects the life I am already living: rooted in the Church, fully engaged in the world through a demanding professional context. I am aware that public ministry carries a cost — holding together family life, professional responsibility and ministry requires discernment and boundaries. Over time this calling has become less something I enjoy and more something I cannot ignore.

Fruitfulness

Shares God's story · Imaginative and relevant · Shares God's love in mission · Good self-care

The clearest fruit I have seen has come through opening scripture with others — preaching, leading Bible studies and facilitating conversation. When I prepare a talk I am always asking: what does this say to someone living a real life this week? I do not want simply to speak well; I want people to leave more able to trust God and articulate their faith.
My most recent sermon — on Romans 15:13, our verse of the year, "Filled, Steeped, Overflowing" — illustrates how I try to connect scripture with everyday life. Beginning with an ordinary cup of tea spilled while opening a door, I used the image of overflow to explore what it means for hope to spill naturally from a Christian into the world around them. The challenge to the congregation was direct: where is your splash zone? Not necessarily a huge splash — perhaps a word quietly spoken, a moment of patience not planned, a conversation that went further than expected. I described it as God choosing to let his hope enter the world through ordinary cups carried by ordinary people. Several people stopped to talk afterwards. One told me it had reframed how they thought about their week.
The fruit I value most, however, is often the slowest and least visible. A close friend joined me on the Cuillin Ridge climb, raising money for Christian Aid — without any personal faith at the time. Over the following years, through a period working abroad and then regular conversations over the Bible when he returned, he began to ask deeper questions about life and meaning. He now attends his local church, and his family have begun to join him. That journey — slow, relational, unforced — is a reminder that faith often grows through shared life and trust rather than through a single moment.
I also see fruitfulness through the men's breakfast, home groups and workplace faith networks — settings where people have grown in confidence, becoming more able to articulate and live out their own faith. My strengths lie in teaching, facilitating and enabling. I resource myself through Bible reading, Christian teaching, time outdoors and relationships that challenge and encourage me. Good self-care is not optional; it is part of faithful ministry.
